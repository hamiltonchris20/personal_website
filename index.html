Research

I am interested in the motion of complex astrophysical systems. I use modern tools from Hamiltonian mechanics, non-equilibrium statistical mechanics, plasma physics and kinetic theory as well as numerical simulations to understand the dynamics of black hole binaries, star clusters, galaxies, etc.

In my PhD I have developed a general secular theory for the dynamical evolution of any binary orbiting an arbitrary axisymmetric potential. The formalism is applicaable to a wide variety of astrophysical systems and also brings several classic problems under a simple unified framework: for instance, (i) the hierarchical three-body problem and (ii) the problem of Oort comets torqued by the Galactic tide both arise as special cases of the general secular theory (Hamilton & Rafikov 2019a, MNRAS). The theory demonstrates that large-amplitude eccentricity oscillations typified by the Lidov-Kozai mechanism --- and often invoked to explain e.g. black hole mergers and hot jupiter formation --- are in fact quite general whenever a wide binary orbits an axisymmetric host system, such as a globular cluster (Hamilton & Rafikov 2019b, MNRAS). Cluster-tide driven eccentricity excitation constitutes a new merger channel for the black hole and neutron star binary mergers currently being detected by LIGO/Virgo (Hamilton & Rafikov 2019c, ApJL).


I am also very interested in the role of collective effects in the long-term evolution of stellar clusters and galaxies. The traditional (Spitzer-Chandrasekhar) picture of the slow evolution of stellar systems says that stars’ orbits are only modified by occasional, uncorrelated, two-body flyby encounters with other stars. However, the long-range nature of the Newtonian gravitational force means that in reality stars can behave collectively. For instance, spherical stellar clusters are highly susceptible to the excitation of system-scale ‘sloshing’ modes. These are density waves — involving many stars at once — that oscillate back and forth much like a sound wave echoes between the walls of a tunnel. The spiral arms of the Milky Way constitute another example of such collective density waves. The question is: what effect do collective oscillations have on the long-term (∼ Gyr or more) dynamical evolution of clusters and galaxies? In an attempt to answer this question I applied the gravitational version of the Balescu-Lenard (BL) kinetic equation to spherical stellar systems (Hamilton et al 2018, MNRAS). My results suggested that global collective oscillations contribute significantly to the slow evolution of globular clusters, a finding which was then partially confirmed numerically (Lau & Binney 2019). If correct, this represents a paradigmatic shift in the canonical picture of cluster relaxation that has persisted since the 1940s.

Furthermore, there are very strong parallels between the kinetic theory of stellar systems and that of plasmas, but theoretical developments in the former are lagging behind by half a century. One well-developed technique in plasma theory is the diagrammatic/‘quasiparticle’ approach to kinetic turbulence. Recently I borrowed this technique and used it to re-derive the BL equation for stellar systems (Hamilton 2020). My derivation is much shorter and simpler than the others given in the literature so far, and also nicely clarifies the connection between the BL theory and the traditional Spitzer-Chandrasekhar approach.
